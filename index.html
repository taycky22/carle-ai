Why the front door question is architectural, not aesthetic

The front door is decided by where people already are. The strategic question isn't which tool is the front door — it's where the AI sits that decides which tool to call. [...]

If a vendor owns it

  • Every AI question goes through their pricing, their schedule, their model choice.
  • Their rules sit next to — or against — Carle's own AI rules.
  • Switching costs grow every year. Every custom thing built is locked in.
  • Connecting their AI to another vendor's AI requires their permission.

If Carle owns it

  • Users ask once, in Teams or Epic. Our AI decides which system to call.
  • We pay AI providers directly, at cost. We pick the model.
  • Vendor agents become helpers we call — not the boss.
  • Our connectors work everywhere, not just one vendor's surface.
The principle

Mental Scorecard
Five tests every vendor pitch should pass
1
Is the data the vendor's, not ours?
Sana for Workday operates on Workday's own data. Sana Enterprise wants to operate on data we own. Different categories.
2
Is the workflow inside the vendor's surface, where users already are?
A clinician in Epic is in Epic. A business user is in Teams, not Workday.
3
Is the regulatory or compliance lift meaningful and vendor-specific?
FDA pathways and HIPAA-specific architecture, yes. Standard business automation, no.

Payroll, tax filing, clinical evidence libraries — yes. Knowledge lookup over our SharePoint — no.
5
Is the switching cost in three to five years manageable?
Changing a connector is fine. Rebuilding every custom agent inside their platform is not.
