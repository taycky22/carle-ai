The Architecture

Vendor systems do what vendor systems do best. The Carle Orchestrator is the connective tissue between them — and the front door for everyone using AI.

About the Orchestrator
Governed front door. Owned by Carle. Connects everything else.
  • MCP, A2A, and APIs — speaks every protocol the vendor agents speak, and a few they don't yet.
  • Doesn't preclude vendor-to-vendor connections where they make sense — just gives Carle the option not to depend on them.
  • Governed and cost-controlled through Microsoft E5 and Azure AI Foundry. Existing tooling, existing security posture.
  • Maintained, extended, and supported by Carle's full-stack development team. No vendor consulting required for changes.
  • Issue isolation, model selection, cost control — the levers that vendor agent platforms don't expose to customers.
  • Reduces "AI-as-FTE-config" pressure — the orchestrator handles the prompt engineering and translation work that vendor pitches typically push onto Carle staff.
The picture in one sentence
Vendor systems do their vertical work deeply. The Carle Orchestrator sits where they overlap — the connecting tissue, the front door, and the layer where the cost and capability levers live. Without it, Carle is a collection of vendor agents that don't talk to each other.
Diagram 02

Why this matters

Six reasons the orchestration layer is worth building and protecting — at executive altitude, without the technical detail.

$

We control the AI bill, not the vendor

Every vendor selling us an "AI agent" is reselling the same underlying AI models we can access directly. When we own the orchestration layer, we choose which model handles which task — cheap models for simple work, premium models only where they matter. Vendors charge a flat rate either way and keep the difference.

⚙

One governed front door instead of a dozen

Without a central layer, every vendor agent has its own login, its own rules, its own data access, and its own AI policies. The orchestration layer lets cyber, compliance, and IT govern AI once — at the front door — instead of negotiating with every vendor separately every time a new agent shows up.

↺

Freedom to change our minds

Vendors release new AI capabilities every few months. Some land well; some don't. Some vendors get acquired; others change pricing dramatically. The orchestration layer lets us swap pieces underneath without disrupting the people using AI day to day. Without it, every vendor switch is an enterprise-scale project.

⚡

A force multiplier for a lean team

Each connector we build — to Workday, Epic, Ivanti, SharePoint — becomes reusable infrastructure. The fifth AI agent costs a fraction of the first one because the connectors already exist. Vendor agent platforms show no such compounding — the fifth vendor agent costs as much as the first.

⌂

One consistent experience for staff and patients

Without orchestration, every system has its own AI assistant — staff learn five different chatbots, patients encounter different AI behaviors across MyChart, scheduling, and billing. The orchestration layer is what makes "Carle AI" feel like one thing instead of five vendors' best guesses.

⟶

Strategic optionality as AI keeps changing

AI capability is moving fast and no one knows exactly where it lands in three years. The orchestration layer is how we stay positioned to take advantage of whatever comes next — new models, new patterns, new vendors — without being locked into decisions we made when the technology looked different.

What it costs us not to have it
Vendors charge full retail for AI they're reselling. Every system has its own ungoverned AI front door. Every vendor switch is a major project. Connectors get rebuilt every time. Staff and patients get five different AI experiences. And we have no leverage when AI capability shifts. The orchestration layer isn't an additional cost — it's what makes everything else affordable, governable, and changeable.

The Six-Step Playbook

If the architecture is right, this is the operational sequence that follows. Roughly the order things happen in — though several run in parallel.

1
Foundation

Continue centralizing Carle knowledge in SharePoint

SharePoint is the working surface for business knowledge — the place where policies, procedures, and process documentation live and stay current. Without a centralized, governed knowledge base, AI agents have nothing to read. Every other step depends on this one continuing.

2
Prioritization

Identify key areas of augmentation need — keep a rolling priority list

Maintain a business-prioritized queue of roughly ten augmentation candidates, updated monthly. Discipline beats opportunism here. The list creates a steady cadence of high-value work and prevents the team from being pulled into one-off vendor pitches that don't compound. Every quarter, ask: is the top of the list actually the highest-leverage thing we could do?

3
Vendor Strategy

Identify where vendors are most mature — buy vertical, not horizontal

Each major vendor has areas where their AI is genuinely mature and areas where it's marketing. Buy the vertical depth. Don't buy the horizontal reach. Workday's payroll agent inside Workday is fair game; Workday as the orchestration layer across non-Workday systems isn't. Same rule applies to every vendor.

4
Process Discipline

Review, document, and optimize processes around what we'll buy, build, or augment

Before adopting a vendor agent or building a Carle agent, the underlying process needs to be documented and intentionally designed — not just captured as it currently runs. Documenting workarounds and then automating them perfectly is worse than no automation. Process owners commit to the target state, not just the current state.

5
Architecture

For everything that doesn't play together well — build agent-to-agent connections

Where systems are redundant, where vendor pricing is unreasonable, or where committing further would mortgage too much of Carle's flexibility, the answer is build a Carle agent that talks to their agent. Lighter than a direct integration, easier to maintain, and portable when the underlying system changes or gets replaced.

6
Knowledge Pipeline

Refine SharePoint knowledge into Dataverse — only clean data reaches Workday

Raw business knowledge starts in SharePoint. Once a process or rule is fully evolved, it gets structured in Dataverse. Only then does it touch Workday's systems of record. Workday stays squeaky clean — only refined data and processes touch the Workday agents. Minimizes risk. Minimizes the training burden on the Workday team. Keeps canonical data canonical.

The reading order
These aren't strictly sequential — Steps 1, 2, and 6 are continuous. Steps 3, 4, and 5 happen per use case as opportunities arise. The discipline isn't doing them in order. It's making sure none of them get skipped.
