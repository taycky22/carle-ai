Section 01

Where the cost actually comes from

AI feels like a soft cost in early conversations. It becomes a hard cost the moment any agent goes into production — and the bill scales with usage. Most enterprise AI sales conversations leave executives with no real picture of what the recurring cost looks like, because vendors structure their pricing to obscure exactly that.

The good news: AI pricing has clearer mechanics than most enterprise software. Once you understand the four cost levers — and which ones we control — the financial picture stops being mysterious.

Routing matters more than tier prices. An organization that always uses the premium tier pays premium rates. An organization that routes commodity tasks to commodity models pays a fraction. Routing is only available when you control the orchestration layer.

The vendor charges $X per agent interaction whether the call needed a penny of AI or fifty cents. They keep the difference.

Section 02

Four levers we control

Owning the orchestration layer means owning four cost levers that vendor agent platforms don't expose to customers. Together, these compound. The blended cost of AI at Carle is structurally lower than what equivalent vendor pricing would deliver — not because we negotiated harder, but because we own the levers.

85%

Prompt caching

System context — policies, SOPs, SharePoint knowledge — gets cached at the session level. Each subsequent reuse costs about 10 cents on the dollar compared to full inference. The more we use it, the cheaper it gets.

60%

Model tier routing

Cheap models for high-volume simple tasks. Mid-tier for most agentic work. Premium only for genuinely complex reasoning. Blended cost approaches the cheapest tier — quality stays at premium where it matters. The single biggest lever.

50%

Batch API discount

Non-real-time work — nightly analytics, document processing, bulk enrichment — routed through batch endpoints at half price. No quality penalty. Just requires async-first architecture, which we already have.

∞

Platform compounding

Agent 1 builds connectors. Agents 2, 3, 4 reuse them. By Agent 5, marginal build cost approaches zero. Vendor platforms show no platform effects — Agent 5 in their tool costs as much as Agent 1.

Why this matters for any AI buying decision
When a vendor quotes $X per agent interaction, ask: which of these four levers does this pricing expose to us? If the answer is "none" — and it almost always is — they keep all the savings these levers generate. We pay full retail. The four levers are the difference between AI as commodity utility and AI as recurring vendor margin.
Section 03

Two futures, one architecture

Nobody knows exactly where AI pricing goes over the next five years. The good news for Carle: we don't have to predict it. The architecture we're building wins under either of the two scenarios that experts are debating.

A
Scenario A
Premium AI stays expensive. Commodity AI gets cheap.
  • High-volume tasks route to commodity models. Knowledge lookups approach near-zero marginal cost.
  • Premium inference reserved for cases with calculable ROI: prior auth, complex scheduling, clinical reasoning.
  • Carle's orchestration layer makes the routing decision — vendors can't, because they don't expose model selection.
  • Knowledge centralization compounds: better retrieval means smaller context means lower cost per interaction.
✓ Carle wins through cost control by architecture, not vendor negotiation
B
Scenario B
All AI gets cheaper. Premium capability democratizes.
  • Commodity layer migrates toward zero cost. Freed budget reinvested in advanced agentic capability.
  • Organizations with owned orchestration move at their own speed. Vendor-dependent organizations move at vendor speed.
  • Workforce transformation accelerates. Role transitions in high-volume positions become economically obvious.
  • Carle's platform advantage compounds — every agent reuses infrastructure that's already paid for.
✓ Carle wins through deployment speed and platform compounding
In either scenario, the routing layer wins.

The Electricity Metaphor
In the early 1900s, factories owned generators. Only large operations could afford the capital cost. When centralized grids arrived, power became commodity — but the industrial machines running on that power stayed expensive. Basic AI inference is becoming the electricity. Agentic capability is the industrial machine. Carle is building the wiring before the grid finishes coming online.

The framing matters: AI eliminates work before it eliminates workers, and the timing differs by category.

Power Grid Constraint

Data center power is triggering grid-level interventions in Virginia, Ireland, Singapore, and the Netherlands. Microsoft paused some data center leases in 2025. AI capacity scaling is now an electricity problem.

HBM Memory Shortage

Large-scale AI inference requires HBM3/HBM3e memory, manufactured almost exclusively by SK Hynix and Samsung. New fab capacity takes 3-4 years to come online. The GPU shortage is downstream of this.

Pacing Incentives

AI vendors derive enterprise revenue from organizations whose budgets are held by the workers being augmented. Soft incentive exists to pace displacement — allowing budget redirection rather than elimination.

Section 05

Three decisions for leadership

If the cost mechanics, the architecture levers, and the workforce tiers all check out, the strategy collapses to three concrete executive decisions. None of these require new technology. All three require institutional permission to act.

1

Act on Tier 1 roles now — with HR in the room

Current AI is sufficient for call center, scheduling coordination, and prior auth prep. Pilot now. Measure honestly. Move to augmentation-and-attrition with HR partnership on the workforce side. Waiting carries a compounding delay cost against health systems already moving.

2

Open the Tier 2 transition window now

The 2-4 year window to retrain mid-level staff into AI operations, governance, and engineering roles is open today. It will not stay open. Identify candidates, fund the transition program, set explicit milestones. The choice is in the timing — not the outcome.

3

Establish AI cost governance before spend reaches the CFO desk

Usage-based AI billing means agent costs reach finance leadership within 12-18 months. Define token budget allocations per agent and per department now. Build the cost dashboards. Make AI spend visible before it becomes a reactive conversation. The governance is cheap; the alternative is expensive surprise.

Vendors are reselling raw AI. We can call the same models directly, route them ourselves, and keep the margin and the model selection. The four levers compound. The architecture wins under either scenario. The workforce transition is a planning problem, not a technology problem.

The closing thought
AI cost isn't a black box once you see the levers. The pricing has clear mechanics. The architecture choices have predictable consequences. The workforce transitions have clear tier boundaries. What's missing isn't information — it's the institutional decision to act on what we already know.
