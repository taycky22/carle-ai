Working with Epic

Where Epic's AI fits, where it doesn't, and how Carle stays in control of clinical AI on Carle's terms — regardless of how Epic's roadmap unfolds.

Section 01

The premise

Epic is the clinical front door at Carle and that's the right answer. Their clinical platform is genuinely exceptional at what it's built to do. The strategic question for Carle isn't whether to use Epic AI — it's how, on whose terms, and with what governance.

Epic is moving aggressively into AI: clinical agents, patient-facing AI (EMMIE), agent builders, and (planned) voice and call center technology. Some of this is genuinely well-architected — open protocols, intelligent model orchestration, allowance for external knowledge bases. Some is Epic stretching beyond its core competency into territory where their track record is mixed.

If AI in clinical workflow is inevitable, we own it. We map it to Carle's clinical standards — not Epic's, with flavors of ours.

That posture is the spine of this document. Partnership where partnership works. Clear lines where it doesn't. And readiness for either outcome.

Section 02

Plan A and Plan B

Epic is building open protocols, intelligent orchestration, and external knowledge support into their AI products. That could turn out great. It also might not. The strategy is built so either outcome works for Carle.

A
Plan A · Optimistic
Epic's open architecture delivers
What it looks like: Epic ships A2A and MCP support on schedule. EMMIE lets Carle plug in our own knowledge bases without forcing data migration. Model swap mechanisms genuinely give us control over which AI Epic uses for our patients.

What we do: Lean into the partnership. Adopt EMMIE for transactional patient interactions where Epic's clinical depth is a real advantage. Keep our orchestration layer talking to Epic as a peer, not a substitute.
B
Plan B · Realistic
Promises slip. Roadmap dates move.
What it looks like: Open protocols are 18 months out, not 6. Knowledge base integration requires Epic-managed migration. Model swap mechanisms turn out to be Epic's choice on Epic's timeline.

What we do: Use Carle's orchestration layer to insulate clinical workflow from Epic-side delays. Adopt narrowly where Epic genuinely delivers; substitute Carle-built capability where they don't. Partnership preserved, but on our terms.
Built so either outcome is fine
The discipline
Hope for Plan A. Build for Plan B. The orchestration layer, the connector library, and the open-protocol adoption all work in either scenario — they're Plan A enablers when Epic ships, and Plan B insurance when they don't. We don't have to predict which scenario plays out. We just have to be ready for both.
Section 03

EMMIE: where to lean in, where to bridge

EMMIE is more architecturally interesting than most vendor AI. Epic is using model orchestration intelligently, building toward open protocols, and allowing external knowledge bases. That's a significantly better posture than the typical vendor-AI playbook. It also means EMMIE is worth treating with the same lens Carle applies to any vertical AI: where does it stay inside its natural strengths, and where is it stretching into territory where Epic doesn't have the track record to back the ambition?

Lean In
Where EMMIE plays to Epic's strengths
  • Patient scheduling. Native Epic data, well-defined transactions, mature workflow. Epic's vertical depth shows.
  • Patient payment workflows. Same logic — transactional, inside Epic's domain, integrated with the billing systems Epic already governs.
  • MyChart-adjacent patient inquiries. Where the patient is already in Epic's ecosystem and the question stays within it.
  • Clinical-context patient questions. Test results, appointment prep, medication questions where Epic's clinical data integration is the genuine differentiator.
Don't Lean In
Where Epic's track record is mixed
  • Front-end patient-facing website. Public web experiences are not Epic's competency. Pure-play vendors have years of head start on patient acquisition and engagement experience.
  • Call center technology. Voice agents are technically harder than text — latency, error recovery, telephony integration. Mature pure-play vendors exist with five years of operational learning.
  • Voice EMMIE specifically. Until proven in production at health systems Carle's size, voice deserves the same scrutiny as any vendor's first-generation product.
  • Anything reaching outside Epic's clinical domain. If EMMIE wants to act on data Epic doesn't govern, that's where Carle's orchestrator does the bridging — not EMMIE reaching out.

The line is whether the use case is transactional inside Epic's natural domain or reaching beyond Epic's clinical-adjacent strengths. Same lens Carle applies to Workday vertical agents. Consistent doctrine, applied to Epic's patient-facing AI.

Section 04

"We built it so we can swap agents"

Epic is signaling that their architecture allows swapping AI models in as new ones progress. That's the right posture and a better-than-average vendor signal. It also has three different versions — and only two of them are genuinely good for Carle. Worth knowing which one Epic actually means before relying on it.

The Three Versions
When a vendor says "we built this to swap agents," they could mean any of these
A
A config flag that lets the customer pick model providers Carle could route EMMIE to Claude, GPT, Gemini, or Epic's default — on Carle's choice. Genuinely customer-controlled. Best version.
B
A versioned agent registry where Carle opts in to newer agents Epic releases new agent versions; Carle decides when to upgrade on our timeline, not Epic's. Genuinely meaningful. Acceptable.
C
Epic decides when to swap agents based on Epic's release cadence "We built this so it's easy for us to ship better agents in the future." Every SaaS vendor does this. Not the architectural advantage Epic implies it is. Familiar territory.
What to ask in the next demo
"What defines an 'agent' in your architecture, and what's the actual mechanism for the swap?" Version A or B is genuinely useful. Version C is Epic's normal product cycle dressed up as architecture. The model lag concern from the primer applies fully if the answer is C.
Section 05

Physician input is signal, not obstacle

In the EMMIE triage demo, physicians on the call pushed back on the AI giving advice. Epic was steadfast — they see this as inevitable. Both can be true: Epic may be directionally right that AI-assisted triage becomes standard, and the physicians may be right that Epic's specific implementation isn't the right answer for Carle.

Why physician hesitation matters
The physicians on that call are the people who'd absorb the consequences of the AI being wrong.
Their hesitation is calibrated by clinical experience that vendors don't have. That makes physician input a calibration signal — not an obstacle to overcome. The question isn't how to convince physicians to accept Epic's design. It's how to design AI-assisted triage that meets Carle's clinical standards, with physician judgment central to the design itself.

"Inevitable" is also a sales frame. Vendors who say "this is inevitable" are usually the ones positioned to benefit if customers accept the inevitability and start adopting on the vendor's schedule. The clinical liability questions — who's liable when the AI is wrong, what guardrails are required, who's accountable on record — are real and unresolved. They deserve serious work before adoption, not after.

The position to hold
If AI in clinical workflow is inevitable, Carle adopts it on Carle's clinical standards and Carle's governance — not Epic's, with flavors of ours layered on. We use their tooling where it fits our standards. We substitute our own where it doesn't. That's a stronger argument than blanket resistance.
Section 06

The skilling reality

Every vendor agent platform requires staff to invest in vendor-specific skills. Carle is too lean to fund that math across multiple vendor stacks. Finding developers and product managers with the time to keep up with AI advances and earn the required vendor certifications — at mid-range pay — is rare. Building with raw tools concentrates skill investment on durable, transferable capabilities instead.

Transferable
Skills that survive vendor transitions and grow more valuable
  • General Python and modern engineering practices
  • Azure AI Foundry orchestration
  • Claude Code and similar raw AI development
  • MCP, A2A, and open-protocol integration
  • Knowledge engineering and RAG architecture
Vendor-Locked
Skills that don't transfer outside the vendor's platform
  • Vendor-specific agent builder certifications
  • Vendor-specific automation tooling
  • Vendor-specific configuration languages
  • Custom integration patterns inside vendor walled gardens
  • Anything that disappears the moment the vendor shifts strategy

This isn't a knock on any single vendor — it's structural. Every vendor's agent platform is its own learning curve. A small team funding multiple vendor specializations is choosing redundant cost over compounding capability. Lean teams have to be ruthless about which skills compound.

What this means for Epic specifically
Adopting Epic vertical AI where it fits Carle's standards is fine — that doesn't require new specialized headcount. Adopting Epic agent builders deeply means investing in Epic-specific AI tooling skills across the dev team, on top of everything else they're already responsible for. We can't afford that math, and we don't have to — the orchestration layer plus Carle-built agents covers most of what Epic agent builders would offer, with skills that transfer.
Section 07

What a good Epic relationship looks like

If we get this right, here's what the Epic relationship should look like in 18 months:

  • EMMIE adopted in scheduling, payments, and clinical-context patient inquiries — where Epic's vertical depth genuinely earns its place.
  • Voice and call center handled by purpose-built tooling, with Carle's orchestrator coordinating across Epic and the voice/call vendors as peers.
  • Epic agent builder usage scoped narrowly to clinical workflows where deep Epic-native context is the differentiator. Not extrapolated into general use.
  • Open-protocol integration in production — Carle's agents and Epic's agents talking via A2A and MCP, with model selection and cost levers staying in Carle's hands.
  • Physician input central to clinical AI design — not as a hurdle to clear, but as the calibration source for what Carle considers safe and useful.
  • Skills concentrated in transferable, durable capabilities — Foundry, raw model APIs, MCP/A2A — not spread across multiple vendor-specific tooling stacks.

If clinical AI is inevitable, Carle owns it. Mapped to our standards. Built to survive whichever way Epic's roadmap unfolds.

The closing thought
Epic's clinical platform is exceptional and the partnership matters. The strategy isn't anti-Epic — it's about preserving Carle's ability to make clinical AI decisions on Carle's terms, with the architecture in place to honor partnership when Epic delivers and to substitute when they don't. Plan A, Plan B, same readiness.
